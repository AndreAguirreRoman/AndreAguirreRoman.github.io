Yuan Mei (1716–1798)
傳 · About the Poet
Yuan Mei was a Qing Dynasty poet, scholar, and gastronome. As described in I Don’t Bow to Buddhas, he was “a feminist, a democrat, a gourmet and a ‘food writer,’ a sensualist and a family man.” Though he passed the rigorous imperial examinations at a young age, he grew disillusioned with bureaucratic life. In his thirties, he retired to Nanjing, where he built the famous "Garden of Contentment" (Suiyuan).

Throughout his life, he was “extraordinarily influential as a poet, literary critic, and poetry teacher,” and is considered “the last great poet of the classical Chinese tradition.” His works, often written in a more natural, spoken style, appealed to both elite scholars and ordinary readers of his time.

He championed the Xingling (Native Sensibility) movement, arguing that poetry should flow from genuine, spontaneous emotion rather than rigid classical rules. His philosophy reflects a balance of traditions—what the introduction describes through the idea that “The Three Ways are One,” blending Confucian, Taoist, and Buddhist influences.

Yuan Mei was fiercely independent. He rejected superstition and rigid orthodoxy, and “rejected forms of Buddhist belief and practice” that lacked sincerity. He accepted female students (scandalous at the time), collected ghost stories, and wrote one of history's most celebrated cookbooks, leaving behind a legacy that combined intellect, individuality, and artistic freedom.

Source: Arthur Waley, I Don’t Bow to Buddhas, Introduction.
